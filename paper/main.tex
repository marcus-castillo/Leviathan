\begin{abstract}
We present \textbf{Leviathan}, an open-source, containerized pipeline for the statistical analysis of
judicial opinions. Leviathan ingests court opinions and their metadata, segments Supreme Court
opinions into majority, concurrence, and dissent components, and computes a battery of descriptive,
lexical, embedding-, and graph-based measures: outcome and tone statistics, majority-versus-dissent
lexical divergence via the weighted log-odds-ratio with an informative Dirichlet prior, topic models,
per-justice writing-style embeddings, and citation-network influence and community structure. The
system is built around an explicit \emph{ethics-first} design: every figure is emitted with a
confidence score, a sample-size warning, and a limitations statement, and the software is constrained
to report statistical disparities only---never claims of intent, ideology, or bias. We validate the
language- and label-producing components against gold codings from the Supreme Court Database (SCDB):
issue-area agreement of \FILL{accuracy/$\kappa$} and outcome agreement of \FILL{accuracy/$\kappa$} over
\FILL{$N$} cases. All code, example data, dataset-versioning tooling, and continuous-integration
configuration are publicly available to support reproduction and reuse.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and Statement of Need}
Empirical study of judicial behavior increasingly draws on natural language processing, yet much of
this work relies on bespoke, unpublished pipelines whose preprocessing, labeling, and aggregation
choices are difficult to inspect or reproduce. Two needs follow. First, the field needs
\emph{shared, auditable infrastructure}: a documented pipeline from raw opinions to analysis-ready
measures, with versioned datasets and tests. Second, because measures of ``judicial bias'' are
ethically fraught and easily over-interpreted, the field needs tooling whose design makes
over-claiming structurally difficult.

Leviathan addresses both. It is a four-component system---(i) a bias-metrics API and dashboard,
(ii) a dataset-standardization CLI with semantic versioning and reproducibility logs, (iii) a Neo4j
citation-graph service with influence and community analysis, and (iv) a Supreme Court analysis
platform---sharing a common embedding space and a common ethics layer. This paper describes the
software, its methods, and a validation of its label-producing components against authoritative
human codings.

\paragraph{Scope and claims.} This is a \emph{software/resource} contribution. We do not claim to
detect bias, intent, or ideology. We claim a reproducible pipeline whose component measures we
validate against gold labels, and whose design enforces caveated, disparity-only reporting.

\section{Related Work}
Quantitative analysis of judicial opinions spans political science and legal NLP. The Supreme Court
Database \cite{spaeth2023scdb} provides authoritative case-level codings (winning party, decision
direction, issue area) and is the standard ground truth for SCOTUS empirical work; ideal-point models
such as Martin--Quinn scores \cite{martin2002dynamic} estimate ideological position from votes. For
distinctive-vocabulary analysis we adopt the weighted log-odds-ratio with an informative Dirichlet
prior of Monroe, Colaresi, and Quinn \cite{monroe2008fightin}. Document and sentence representations
build on sentence embeddings \cite{reimers2019sentencebert} and domain language models
\cite{chalkidis2020legalbert}; legal-outcome prediction has been studied since at least
\cite{aletras2016predicting}. We rely on established components---spaCy \cite{honnibal2020spacy},
scikit-learn \cite{pedregosa2011scikit}, non-negative matrix factorization \cite{lee1999nmf}, and
Louvain community detection \cite{blondel2008louvain}---and on open corpora from the Free Law Project
(CourtListener) \cite{freelaw} and the Caselaw Access Project \cite{cap}. Our contribution is not a
new algorithm but the integration, validation, and ethics-constrained packaging of these methods.

\section{System Architecture}
Leviathan is a polyglot monorepo orchestrated with Docker Compose. A FastAPI backend exposes the
bias-metrics API; a PostgreSQL instance with the \texttt{pgvector} extension stores opinions,
analyses, and 384-dimensional sentence embeddings (\texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}). The \texttt{levcorpus}
CLI produces standardized JSONL/CSV/Parquet datasets with a semantic-version registry and
reproducibility logs. A Neo4j service stores the citation graph; influence, clustering, and
``statistical grouping'' analytics run on NetworkX projections so they are testable without a live
database. The SCOTUS service adds opinion segmentation and per-justice analysis. Three Next.js
dashboards and a static landing page provide visualization. Continuous integration runs all Python
test suites, byte-compilation, and frontend type-checks.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Opinion segmentation}
SCOTUS opinions are split into majority, concurrence, dissent, and per-curiam segments by detecting
authorship/role headers (e.g.\ ``\textsc{Justice} X delivered the opinion of the Court'';
``\ldots, dissenting'') and attributing the authoring justice. The procedure is a transparent,
auditable heuristic rather than a learned parser; we validate its outputs in
Section~\ref{sec:validation}.

\subsection{Lexical divergence}
To surface vocabulary distinctive of one side of a decision, we compute the weighted log-odds-ratio
with an informative Dirichlet prior \cite{monroe2008fightin}. For word $w$ with counts $y^{(i)}_w$ in
corpus $i\in\{A,B\}$ and prior $\alpha_w$ derived from the combined corpus, the statistic is the
$z$-score of the prior-shrunk log-odds difference; the prior down-weights rare-word noise that raw
frequency and tf--idf do not. We apply this to majority-versus-dissent and to each justice versus
their peers (``framing'').

\subsection{Topic modeling and theme tagging}
We fit non-negative matrix factorization \cite{lee1999nmf} over tf--idf representations to obtain
interpretable topics, and additionally tag opinions with a curated constitutional-theme lexicon whose
assignments trace to matched terms.

\subsection{Justice style embeddings}
A justice's style fingerprint is the mean of their authored-segment sentence embeddings
\cite{reimers2019sentencebert}. We report pairwise cosine similarity and $k$-means style clusters with
\emph{neutral} labels; these are explicitly not ideology scores.

\subsection{Citation graph and influence}
Opinions and their citations form a directed graph with typed edges (cites/follows/overrules/
distinguishes). We compute PageRank, betweenness, and Louvain communities \cite{blondel2008louvain} on
NetworkX projections, and a parameter-sensitive, label-neutral statistical grouping of judges.

\section{Validation against the Supreme Court Database}
\label{sec:validation}
We validate the label-producing components against SCDB \cite{spaeth2023scdb} on the case-centered
release. Opinions are joined to SCDB rows by normalized U.S.\ reporter citation. We report:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Issue/theme agreement}: the top theme from our lexicon tagger versus SCDB
        \texttt{issueArea}, scored over the subset of SCDB areas with a confident crosswalk (the
        crosswalk is a documented modeling decision; ambiguous areas are excluded).
  \item \textbf{Outcome agreement}: our outcome classifier mapped to the SCDB \texttt{partyWinning}
        space (petitioner/respondent), over cases SCDB codes as having a clear winner.
\end{itemize}

\section{Ethics and Limitations}
Leviathan is designed for hypothesis generation under expert review, not adjudication. The software
enforces, in code, that every signal carries a confidence score, a sample-size warning below a
configurable threshold, and a limitations string; narrative text is scrubbed of intent/ideology
vocabulary. Key limitations: (i) outcome, tone, and theme labels are model-generated and noisy;
(ii) corpus coverage is uneven across courts and eras, so absence of data is not absence of activity;
(iii) ``similar'' cases by embedding need not be legally equivalent; (iv) statistical groupings are
artifacts of the corpus, feature set, and parameters and are \emph{not} ideology measures---for
ideology, validated external instruments such as Martin--Quinn \cite{martin2002dynamic} should be
used; (v) nothing here supports causal claims about any judge.

\section{Reproducibility and Availability}
The repository (\url{https://github.com/marcus-castillo/Leviathan}) contains all source, example data,
Docker configuration, and CI. Datasets are versioned via \texttt{levcorpus} with content hashes and
reproducibility logs. \texttt{paper/REPRODUCE.md} maps every reported number to the exact command that
regenerates it. Software environment: \FILL{commit hash, image digests, model versions}.

\section{Conclusion}
Leviathan packages established legal-NLP and graph methods into an auditable, reproducible, and
ethically constrained pipeline, validated against authoritative human codings. We hope it lowers the
barrier to transparent empirical work on judicial text while making over-claiming structurally hard.
