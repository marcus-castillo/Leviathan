\title{Leviathan: An Open, Reproducible Pipeline for Statistical Text Analysis of Judicial Opinions}

\author[ind]{Marcus Castillo}
\ead{marcus.f.castillo@gmail.com}
\address[ind]{\FILL{affiliation / ``Independent Researcher''}}

\begin{abstract}
We present \textbf{Leviathan}, an open-source, containerized pipeline for the statistical analysis of
judicial opinions. Leviathan ingests opinions and metadata, segments U.S. Supreme Court opinions into
majority, concurrence, and dissent components, and computes descriptive, lexical, embedding-, and
graph-based measures: outcome and tone statistics, majority-versus-dissent lexical divergence via the
weighted log-odds-ratio with an informative Dirichlet prior, topic models, per-justice writing-style
embeddings, and citation-network influence and community structure. The system is built around an
explicit \emph{ethics-first} design: every figure is emitted with a confidence score, a sample-size
warning, and a limitations statement, and the software is constrained to report statistical
disparities only---never claims of intent, ideology, or bias. We validate the label-producing
components against gold codings from the Supreme Court Database (SCDB), obtaining issue-area agreement
of \FILL{acc/$\kappa$} and outcome agreement of \FILL{acc/$\kappa$} over \FILL{$N$} cases. All code,
example data, dataset-versioning tooling, and continuous-integration configuration are publicly
available.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation and significance}
Empirical study of judicial behavior increasingly draws on natural language processing, yet much of
this work relies on bespoke, unpublished pipelines whose preprocessing, labeling, and aggregation
choices are difficult to inspect or reproduce. Two needs follow. First, the field needs
\emph{shared, auditable infrastructure}: a documented path from raw opinions to analysis-ready
measures, with versioned datasets and tests. Second, because measures framed around ``judicial bias''
are ethically fraught and easily over-interpreted, the field needs tooling whose design makes
over-claiming structurally difficult.

Leviathan addresses both. It is a four-component system---(i) a bias-metrics API and dashboard,
(ii) a dataset-standardization CLI with semantic versioning and reproducibility logs, (iii) a Neo4j
citation-graph service with influence and community analysis, and (iv) a Supreme Court analysis
platform---sharing a common embedding space and a common ethics layer. Quantitative analysis of
opinions spans political science and legal NLP: the Supreme Court Database \cite{spaeth2023scdb}
supplies authoritative case-level codings and is the standard ground truth for SCOTUS work; ideal-point
models such as Martin--Quinn scores \cite{martin2002dynamic} estimate ideological position from votes;
distinctive-vocabulary analysis follows Monroe, Colaresi, and Quinn \cite{monroe2008fightin}; and legal
text representation builds on sentence embeddings \cite{reimers2019sentencebert} and domain models
\cite{chalkidis2020legalbert}, with outcome prediction studied since \cite{aletras2016predicting}.
Leviathan's contribution is not a new algorithm but the integration, validation, and
ethics-constrained packaging of these established methods into reusable research software.

\paragraph{Scope.} This is a software/resource contribution. We do not claim to detect bias, intent,
or ideology; we claim a reproducible pipeline whose component measures are validated against gold
labels and whose design enforces caveated, disparity-only reporting.

\section{Software description}

\subsection{Software architecture}
Leviathan is a polyglot monorepo orchestrated with Docker Compose. A FastAPI backend exposes the
bias-metrics API; a PostgreSQL instance with the \texttt{pgvector} extension stores opinions,
analyses, and 384-dimensional sentence embeddings (\texttt{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}). The \texttt{levcorpus}
CLI produces standardized JSONL/CSV/Parquet datasets with a semantic-version registry and
reproducibility logs. A Neo4j service stores the citation graph; influence, clustering, and
label-neutral statistical-grouping analytics run on NetworkX projections so they are testable without
a live database. The SCOTUS service adds opinion segmentation and per-justice analysis. Three Next.js
dashboards and a static landing page provide visualization. Continuous integration runs all Python
test suites, byte-compilation, and frontend type-checks.

\subsection{Software functionalities}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Ingestion} of CourtListener and plain-text opinions with metadata, plus idempotent
        upsert and judge/court normalization.
  \item \textbf{Opinion segmentation} into majority, concurrence, dissent, and per-curiam parts via
        auditable authorship/role-header detection, with authoring-justice attribution.
  \item \textbf{Lexical divergence}: the weighted log-odds-ratio with an informative Dirichlet prior
        \cite{monroe2008fightin} for majority-versus-dissent and per-justice ``framing'' vocabulary;
        the prior down-weights rare-word noise that raw frequency and tf--idf do not.
  \item \textbf{Topic modeling} via non-negative matrix factorization \cite{lee1999nmf} over tf--idf,
        plus a curated constitutional-theme lexicon whose tags trace to matched terms.
  \item \textbf{Justice style embeddings}: mean-pooled sentence embeddings
        \cite{reimers2019sentencebert} yielding pairwise similarity and $k$-means style clusters with
        neutral (non-ideological) labels.
  \item \textbf{Citation-graph analytics}: PageRank, betweenness, and Louvain communities
        \cite{blondel2008louvain} over typed citation edges.
  \item \textbf{Ethics layer}: every emitted signal carries a confidence score, a sample-size warning
        below a configurable threshold, and a limitations string; narrative text is programmatically
        scrubbed of intent/ideology vocabulary.
\end{itemize}

\section{Illustrative examples}

\subsection{Validation against the Supreme Court Database}
\label{sec:validation}
We validate the label-producing components against SCDB \cite{spaeth2023scdb} (case-centered release),
joining opinions to SCDB rows by normalized U.S. reporter citation. \emph{Issue/theme agreement}
compares the top theme from our lexicon tagger to SCDB \texttt{issueArea} over the subset of SCDB areas
with a confident crosswalk (the crosswalk is a documented modeling decision; ambiguous areas are
excluded). \emph{Outcome agreement} compares our outcome classifier, mapped to the SCDB
\texttt{partyWinning} space, over cases SCDB codes with a clear winner. All values are produced by
\texttt{scripts/validate\_against\_scdb.py} and read from \texttt{paper/results/scdb\_validation.json};
they are not hand-edited.

\section{Impact}
Leviathan lowers the barrier to transparent, reproducible empirical work on judicial text. By
packaging ingestion, segmentation, validated labeling, embeddings, and graph analytics behind a common
API, dataset-versioning CLI, and tested codebase, it lets researchers reproduce and extend analyses
rather than re-implement pipelines. The validation against authoritative SCDB codings (Section
\ref{sec:validation}) gives users calibrated expectations for the noisy components, and the
ethics layer encodes responsible-reporting norms directly in software---useful for teaching and for
oversight-oriented research where over-claiming carries real cost. The dataset standardizer also
produces analysis-ready corpora reusable beyond this system.

\section{Limitations}
Outcome, tone, and theme labels are model-generated and noisy; corpus coverage is uneven across courts
and eras, so absence of data is not absence of activity; embedding ``similarity'' need not imply legal
equivalence; statistical groupings are artifacts of the corpus, feature set, and parameters and are
\emph{not} ideology measures---for ideology, validated external instruments such as Martin--Quinn
\cite{martin2002dynamic} should be used; and nothing in the system supports causal claims about any
judge. The bundled example corpora are synthetic and exist only for smoke-testing; reported results
must come from real data.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- %
\section{Conclusions}
Leviathan packages established legal-NLP and graph methods into an auditable, reproducible, and
ethically constrained pipeline, validated against authoritative human codings. We hope it lowers the
barrier to transparent empirical work on judicial text while making over-claiming structurally hard.
